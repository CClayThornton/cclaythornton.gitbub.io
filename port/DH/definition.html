Definition

Since the punch-card driven Index Thomisticus (a concordance of writings centered around Thomas Aquinas) started by Roberto Busa in 1949, the Digital Humanities have spiraled into an everchanging field that deeply impacts research, preservation, data visualization, publishing, archiving, interdisciplinary collaboration, social media, accesabilitiy, and much, much more. As stated in Debates in the Digital Humanities published in 2016 from writers Matthew K. Gold and Lauren F. Klein:

“... DH now encompasses a wide range of methods and practices: visualizations of large image sets, 3D modeling of historical artifacts, 'born digital' dissertations, hashtag activism and the analysis thereof, alternate reality games, mobile makerspaces, and more. … it can at times be difficult to determine with any specificity what, precisely, digital humanities work entails.”
